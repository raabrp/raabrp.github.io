For finding local minima of differentiable functions, the work-horse algorithm of machine learning is gradient descent.

Gradient descent is really a special case of a wider family of non-linear optimization algorithms, and recognizing this fact allows us to address constraints, consider alternative metrics, and be more explicit about the approximations we use and the sequence of subproblems we solve.

The most basic form of gradient descent, where we seek to minimize an objective function f ⁣:X→Rf \colon \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}f:X→R over decision variable x∈Xx \in \mathcal{X}x∈X, is

xt+1=xt−η∇f(xt),\begin{equation} \label{eqn-1}\tag{1} x_{t+1} = x_{t} - \eta \nabla f(x_{t}), \end{equation}xt+1​=xt​−η∇f(xt​),​(1)​

where η>0\eta > 0η>0 is some step-size that we must choose.

This equation is the solution that minimizes a local approximation of fff plus a regularization term DDD that penalizes us for choosing xxx that results in large updates. That is, gradient descent generates a sequence of candidate solutions xtx_{t}xt​ by solving a sequence of subproblems of the form

xt+1=argminx[f^t(x)+Dt(x)].\begin{equation} \label{eqn-2}\tag{2} x_{t+1} = \underset{x}{\rm argmin}\bigg[\hat{f}_{t}(x) + D_{t}(x) \bigg]. \end{equation}xt+1​=xargmin​[f^​t​(x)+Dt​(x)].​(2)​

Specifically, Eq. (1) solves

xt+1=argminx[f(xt)+⟨∇f(xt),x−xt⟩1η⏟f^t(x)+12η∥x−xt∥22⏟Dt(x)].\begin{equation*} x_{t+1} = \underset{x}{\rm argmin}\bigg[\underbrace{f(x_{t}) + \big\langle \nabla f(x_{t}), x - x_{t} \big\rangle \vphantom{\frac{1}{\eta}}}_{\hat{f}_{t}(x)} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2 \eta} \big\| x - x_{t}\big\|^{2}_{2}}_{D_{t}(x)} \bigg]. \end{equation*}xt+1​=xargmin​[f^​t​(x)f(xt​)+⟨∇f(xt​),x−xt​⟩η1​​​+Dt​(x)2η1​∥∥​x−xt​∥∥​22​​​].​

There are a number of generalizations we can make to Eq. (2). For example,

  • We may choose non-Euclidean update metrics DtD_{t}Dt​.
  • We may choose DtD_{t}Dt​ based on multiple past iterates.
  • We may choose non-local estimates f^t\hat{f}_{t}f^​t​.
  • We may replace fff with a Lagrangian to address constraints.

In the following sections, I give examples of each of these generalizations.

Pseudo-Riemannian Geometry

Let us consider linear approximations of the form

f^t(x)=f(xt)+⟨∇f(xt),x−xt⟩,\begin{equation*} \hat{f}_{t}(x) = f(x_{t}) + \big\langle \nabla f(x_{t}), x - x_{t} \big\rangle, \end{equation*}f^​t​(x)=f(xt​)+⟨∇f(xt​),x−xt​⟩,​

and distances DtD_{t}Dt​ of the form

Dt(x)=12(x−xt)⊤Gt(x−xt),\begin{equation} \label{eqn-3}\tag{3} D_{t}(x) = \frac{1}{2} (x - x_{t})^{\top} G_{t} (x - x_{t}), \end{equation}Dt​(x)=21​(x−xt​)⊤Gt​(x−xt​),​(3)​

where GtG_{t}Gt​ is a (pseudo)metric on the tangent space of xxx.

Under these conditions, it follows that

xt+1=xt−Gt†∇f(xt),\begin{equation} \label{eqn-4}\tag{4} x_{t+1} = x_{t} - G^{\dagger}_{t} \nabla f(x_{t}), \end{equation}xt+1​=xt​−Gt†​∇f(xt​),​(4)​

where Gt†G^{\dagger}_{t}Gt†​ is the Moore-Penrose (pseudo)inverse of GtG_{t}Gt​. This update rule is commonly known as “preconditioned” gradient descent.

Importantly, these conditions correspond to the limit of updates in continuous-time for the true objective fff and any DtD_{t}Dt​ with positive-semidefinite Hessian, wherein

∇[f(x)+D˙t(x)](x=xt)=0\begin{equation*} \nabla \bigg[f(x) + \dot{D}_{t}(x) \bigg]_{(x=x_{t})} = 0 \end{equation*}∇[f(x)+D˙t​(x)](x=xt​)​=0​

(Amid and Warmuth (2020)). To see this, let

Gt=∂2∂x2Dt(x)  ⟹  ∇D˙t(x)=x˙∂2∂x2Dt(x)=Gtx˙.\begin{equation*} G_{t} = \frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial x^{2}} D_{t}(x) \quad \implies \quad \nabla \dot{D}_{t}(x) = \dot{x} \frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial x^{2}} D_{t}(x) = G_{t} \dot{x}. \end{equation*}Gt​=∂x2∂2​Dt​(x)⟹∇D˙t​(x)=x˙∂x2∂2​Dt​(x)=Gt​x˙.​

It follows that

x˙t=−Gt†∇f(xt).\begin{align*} \dot{x}_{t} = - G_{t}^{\dagger} \nabla f(x_{t}). \end{align*}x˙t​=−Gt†​∇f(xt​).​

Note that we use the dot (e.g., over xtx_{t}xt​ and DtD_{t}Dt​) to represent a time-derivative.

Gauss-Newton

If our objective function fff is convex, we are guaranteed that the Hessian of fff, which we denote as HHH, is positive semi-definite. That is,

Ht=∂2∂x2f(xt)⪰0.\begin{equation*} H_{t} = \frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial x^{2}} f(x_{t}) \succeq 0. \end{equation*}Ht​=∂x2∂2​f(xt​)⪰0.​

When we substitute HtH_{t}Ht​ for GtG_{t}Gt​ in Eq. (3), then the resulting subproblem (Eq. (2)) is simply a minimization over a 2nd-order Taylor approximation of fff at xtx_{t}xt​. The resulting update is known as Gauss-Newton and is frequently used for solving least-squares problems (since, as a sum-of-squares, fff is convex).

Fisher-Rao

We may also choose values of GGG that derive from spaces other than X\mathcal{X}X. For example, when xxx parameterizes a probability distribution ρ(y;x)\rho(y ; x)ρ(y;x), from which the objective function fff derives, we may measure the magnitude of an update from xtx_{t}xt​ to xxx by the relative entropy from ρt\rho_{t}ρt​ to ρ\rhoρ.

Dt(x)=1ηDKL(ρ(y;x)∥ρ(y;xt))=1η∫Yρ(y;x)log⁡ρ(y;x)ρ(y;xt)dy.\begin{equation*} D_{t}(x) = \frac{1}{\eta} D_{\rm KL}\Big(\rho(y ; x) \parallel \rho(y ; x_{t})\Big) = \frac{1}{\eta} \int_{\mathcal{Y}} \rho(y ; x) \log \frac{\rho(y; x)}{\rho(y ; x_{t})} {\rm d}y. \end{equation*}Dt​(x)=η1​DKL​(ρ(y;x)∥ρ(y;xt​))=η1​∫Y​ρ(y;x)logρ(y;xt​)ρ(y;x)​dy.​

This choice yields the update rule for Fisher-Rao natural gradient descent:

xt+1=xt−ηFt†∇f(xt),\begin{equation} \label{eqn-5}\tag{5} x_{t+1} = x_{t} - \eta F^{\dagger}_{t} \nabla f(x_{t}), \end{equation}xt+1​=xt​−ηFt†​∇f(xt​),​(5)​

where FtF_{t}Ft​ is the Fisher metric tensor

Ft=∫Yρ(y;xt)∂2∂x2log⁡ρ(y;x)dy.\begin{equation*} F_{t} = \int_{\mathcal{Y}} \rho(y ; x_{t}) \frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial x^{2}} \log \rho(y ; x) {\rm d}y. \end{equation*}Ft​=∫Y​ρ(y;xt​)∂x2∂2​logρ(y;x)dy.​

One way to understand the effect of the metric G=FG = FG=F is that it (un)warps the space of marginal updates dx{\rm d}xdx, such that updates of the same induced magnitude contain the same amount of marginal information about ρ(xt+dx)\rho(x_{t} + {\rm d}x)ρ(xt​+dx).

In continuous time, Fisher-Rao natural gradient descent optimally approximates replicator dynamics (used to model evolution by natural selection) and continuous Bayesian inference (Raab et. al. (2022)). We may also choose different divergences in ρ\rhoρ space, yielding other forms of “natural gradient descent” (Nurbekyan et. al. (2022)).

Multi-iterate Methods

So far, we have only discussed updates that rely on at most one previous iterate of xtx_{t}xt​ (or λt\lambda_{t}λt​ or μt\mu_{t}μt​). We may also choose update penalties DtD_{t}Dt​, that rely on additional history of the solution candidates (xt,xt−1,...)(x_{t}, x_{t-1}, ...)(xt​,xt−1​,...).

Momentum

An example of a multi-iterate approach includes a “momentum”-based method. Consider, for example, a penalty term DtD_{t}Dt​ that quadratically penalizes step-sizes with a linear term that encourages steps in same continued direction, as in

Dt(x)=1η(12∥x−xt∥22−ξ⟨x−xt,xt−xt−1⟩),\begin{equation*} D_{t}(x) = \frac{1}{\eta} \bigg( \frac{1}{2} \big\| x - x_{t} \big\|_{2}^{2} - \xi \big\langle x - x_{t}, x_{t} - x_{t-1} \big\rangle \bigg), \end{equation*}Dt​(x)=η1​(21​∥∥​x−xt​∥∥​22​−ξ⟨x−xt​,xt​−xt−1​⟩),​

where ξ∈(0,1)\xi \in (0, 1)ξ∈(0,1). Alternatively, consider a quadratic penalty for the difference between the proposed update and the previous update with decay ξ\xiξ, as in

Dt(x)=12η∥(x−xt)−ξ(xt−xt−1)∥22.\begin{equation*} D_{t}(x) = \frac{1}{2 \eta} \big\| (x - x_{t}) - \xi (x_{t} - x_{t-1}) \big\|_{2}^{2}. \end{equation*}Dt​(x)=2η1​∥∥​(x−xt​)−ξ(xt​−xt−1​)∥∥​22​.​

In either case,

∇Dt(x)=1η((x−xt)−ξ(xt−xt−1)).\begin{equation*} \nabla D_{t}(x) = \frac{1}{\eta} \bigg((x - x_{t}) - \xi (x_{t} - x_{t-1})\bigg). \end{equation*}∇Dt​(x)=η1​((x−xt​)−ξ(xt​−xt−1​)).​

If we solve Eq. (2) by setting ∇(f^t+Dt)=0\nabla (\hat{f}_{t} + D_{t}) = 0∇(f^​t​+Dt​)=0 for either choice of DtD_{t}Dt​, the resulting update rule is

xt+1=xt+ξ(xt−xt−1)−η∇f^t,\begin{equation*} x_{t+1} = x_{t} + \xi(x_{t} - x_{t-1}) - \eta \nabla \hat{f}_{t}, \end{equation*}xt+1​=xt​+ξ(xt​−xt−1​)−η∇f^​t​,​

which can be decomposed, with an additional state variable vvv, to

vt+1=ξvt−η∇f^t,xt+1=xt+vt+1.\begin{equation} \label{eqn-6}\tag{6} \begin{aligned} v_{t+1} &= \xi v_{t} - \eta \nabla \hat{f}_{t}, \\ x_{t+1} &= x_{t} + v_{t+1}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}vt+1​xt+1​​=ξvt​−η∇f^​t​,=xt​+vt+1​.​​(6)​

This update rule is the classical “Momentum” algorithm (Botev et. al. (2016)) with decay parameterized by ξ\xiξ.

Nth-Order Regularization

There is yet another update penalty DtD_{t}Dt​ that may be used to derive the Momentum update rule, up to a reparameterization of constants (ξ1,ξ2)↦ξ(\xi_{1}, \xi_{2}) \mapsto \xi(ξ1​,ξ2​)↦ξ. Specifically, let

Dt(x)=ξ12η∥x−xt∥2+ξ22η∥(x−xt)⏟v−(xt−xt−1)⏟vt∥2.\begin{equation*} D_{t}(x) = \frac{\xi_{1}}{2\eta} \big\| x - x_{t} \big\|^{2} + \frac{\xi_{2}}{2\eta} \big\| \underbrace{(x - x_{t})}_{v} - \underbrace{(x_{t} - x_{t-1})}_{v_{t}} \big\|^{2}. \end{equation*}Dt​(x)=2ηξ1​​∥∥​x−xt​∥∥​2+2ηξ2​​∥∥​v(x−xt​)​​−vt​(xt​−xt−1​)​​∥∥​2.​

We can naturally extend this distance penalty to account for higher-order derivatives as

Dt(x)=ξ12η∥x−xt∥2+ξ22η∥v−vt∥2+...+ξn2η∥x(n)∥2,\begin{equation*} D_{t}(x) = \frac{\xi_{1}}{2\eta} \big\|x - x_{t} \big\|^{2} + \frac{\xi_{2}}{2\eta} \big\| v - v_{t} \big\|^{2} + ... + \frac{\xi_{n}}{2\eta} \big\| x^{(n)} \big\|^{2}, \end{equation*}Dt​(x)=2ηξ1​​∥∥​x−xt​∥∥​2+2ηξ2​​∥∥​v−vt​∥∥​2+...+2ηξn​​∥∥​x(n)∥∥​2,​

where we use the state variable x(n)x^{(n)}x(n) to represent the nnnth time-derivative of xxx, defined empirically by the recursive formula

x(n)=x(n−1)−xt(n−1).\begin{equation*} x^{(n)} = x^{(n-1)} - x_{t}^{(n-1)}. \end{equation*}x(n)=x(n−1)−xt(n−1)​.​

By this choice of DtD_{t}Dt​, if we solve Eq. (2) by setting ∇(f^t+Dt)=0\nabla (\hat{f}_{t} + D_{t}) = 0∇(f^​t​+Dt​)=0, we obtain the update rules

xt+1(n−1)=1ξn+ξn−1(ξnxt(n−1)−∑k=1n−2ξkx(k)−η∇f^t),xt+1(n−2)=xt(n−2)+xt+1(n−1),⋮xt+1=xt+vt+1.\begin{equation} \label{eqn-7}\tag{7} \begin{aligned} x^{(n-1)}_{t+1} &= \frac{1}{\xi_{n} + \xi_{n-1}} \bigg( \xi_{n} x^{(n-1)}_{t} - \sum_{k=1}^{n-2} \xi_{k} x^{(k)} - \eta \nabla \hat{f}_{t} \bigg), \\ x^{(n-2)}_{t+1} &= x^{(n-2)}_{t} + x^{(n-1)}_{t+1}, \\ &\vdots \\ x_{t+1} &= x_{t} + v_{t+1}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}xt+1(n−1)​xt+1(n−2)​xt+1​​=ξn​+ξn−1​1​(ξn​xt(n−1)​−k=1∑n−2​ξk​x(k)−η∇f^​t​),=xt(n−2)​+xt+1(n−1)​,⋮=xt​+vt+1​.​​(7)​

Non-local Approximation

As xxx varies from xtx_{t}xt​ to xt+1x_{t+1}xt+1​, the minimum average error of a local approximation of f(x)f(x)f(x) along this path can be reduced by choosing an intermediate point x~t+1\tilde{x}_{t+1}x~t+1​, somewhere between xtx_{t}xt​ and xt+1x_{t+1}xt+1​, instead of xtx_{t}xt​, about which to approximate the local behavior of fff.

Nesterov Acceleration

When using Eq. (6), one “zero-cost” (i.e., if we decouple candidate solutions xtx_{t}xt​ from the points at which we query f^\hat{f}f^​) choice of x~t\tilde{x}_{t}x~t​ is given by

x~t+1=xt+ξvt,\begin{equation*} \tilde{x}_{t+1} = x_{t} + \xi v_{t}, \end{equation*}x~t+1​=xt​+ξvt​,​

such that

f^t(x)=f(x~t+1)+⟨∇f(x~t+1),x−xt⟩.\begin{equation} \label{eqn-8}\tag{8} \hat{f}_{t}(x) = f(\tilde{x}_{t+1}) + \big\langle \nabla f(\tilde{x}_{t+1}), x - x_{t} \big\rangle. \end{equation}f^​t​(x)=f(x~t+1​)+⟨∇f(x~t+1​),x−xt​⟩.​(8)​

Substituting Eq. (8) into Eq. (6), we obtain Nesterov Momentum:

vt+1=ξvt−η∇f(xt+ξvt),xt+1=xt+vt+1.\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} v_{t+1} &= \xi v_{t} - \eta \nabla f(x_{t} + \xi v_{t}), \\ x_{t+1} &= x_{t} + v_{t+1}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}vt+1​xt+1​​=ξvt​−η∇f(xt​+ξvt​),=xt​+vt+1​.​​

Dealing with Constraints

Consider the basic (constrained) optimization problem

minimizef(x)subject tog(x)⪰0,h(x)=0,\begin{equation} \begin{array}{lll} {\rm minimize} &f(x) \\ {\rm subject~to} &g(x) \succeq 0, \\ &h(x) = 0, \end{array} \end{equation}minimizesubject to​f(x)g(x)⪰0,h(x)=0,​​​

for vector-valued ggg and hhh.

In terms of the corresponding Lagrangian L\mathcal{L}L, the primal problem may be written

minimizex(maxλ,μL(x,λ,μ))subject toλ⪰0,\begin{equation} \label{eqn-10}\tag{10} \begin{array}{lll} \underset{x}{\rm minimize} \bigg( \underset{\lambda, \mu}{\rm max}& \mathcal{L}(x, \lambda, \mu) \bigg) \\ {\rm subject~to} & \lambda \succeq 0, \end{array} \end{equation}xminimize​(λ,μmax​subject to​L(x,λ,μ))λ⪰0,​​(10)​

where

L(x,λ,μ)=f(x)+λ⊤g(x)+μ⊤h(x).\begin{equation} \mathcal{L}(x, \lambda, \mu) = f(x) + \lambda^{\top} g(x) + \mu^{\top} h(x). \end{equation}L(x,λ,μ)=f(x)+λ⊤g(x)+μ⊤h(x).​​

Intuitively, given that λ\lambdaλ and μ\muμ are chosen adversarially (i.e., after xxx is fixed), the problem is to choose xxx such that the constraints on g(x)g(x)g(x) and h(x)h(x)h(x) are satisfied (otherwise, the objective can be made unboundedly positive by an adversary’s choice of λ,μ\lambda, \muλ,μ). Within this “feasible set” of xxx values, fff should be minimized.

Augmented Lagrangian

We may iteratively approximate Eq. (10) with a sequence of subproblems in the form of Eq. (2):

xt+1=argminx[maxλ≻0,μ (Lt(x,λ,μ)+Dt(x)−Rt(λ)−St(μ))],\begin{equation*} x_{t+1} = \underset{x}{\rm argmin}\bigg[ \underset{\lambda \succ 0, \mu}{\rm max} ~ \bigg( \mathcal{L}_{t}(x, \lambda, \mu) + D_{t}(x) - R_{t}(\lambda) - S_{t}(\mu) \bigg) \bigg], \end{equation*}xt+1​=xargmin​[λ≻0,μmax​ (Lt​(x,λ,μ)+Dt​(x)−Rt​(λ)−St​(μ))],​

with additional update penalties RtR_{t}Rt​ and StS_{t}St​.

This approach is known as an “Augmented Lagrangian Method” (Nocedal & Wright (2006); ch. 17).

Primal-Dual

For the simple choice

Dt(x)=12η∥x−xt∥22,Rt(λ)=12α∥λ−λt∥22,St(μ)=12β∥μ−μt∥22,\begin{align*} D_{t}(x) &= \frac{1}{2\eta}\big\|x - x_{t}\big\|_{2}^{2}, \\ R_{t}(\lambda) &= \frac{1}{2\alpha}\big\|\lambda - \lambda_{t}\big\|_{2}^{2}, \\ S_{t}(\mu) &= \frac{1}{2\beta}\big\|\mu - \mu_{t}\big\|_{2}^{2}, \end{align*}Dt​(x)Rt​(λ)St​(μ)​=2η1​∥∥​x−xt​∥∥​22​,=2α1​∥∥​λ−λt​∥∥​22​,=2β1​∥∥​μ−μt​∥∥​22​,​

and linear local approximation

f^t(x)=f(xt)+⟨∇f(xt),x−xt⟩,\begin{equation*} \hat{f}_{t}(x) = f(x_{t}) + \big\langle \nabla f(x_{t}), x - x_{t} \big\rangle, \end{equation*}f^​t​(x)=f(xt​)+⟨∇f(xt​),x−xt​⟩,​

we have the update rules

xt+1=xt−η(∇f(xt)+λt⊤g(xt)+μt⊤h(xt)),λt+1=max⁡(λt+αg(xt+1),0),μt+1=μt+βh(xt+1).\begin{align*} x_{t+1} &= x_{t} - \eta \bigg( \nabla f(x_{t}) + \lambda_{t}^{\top} g(x_{t}) + \mu^{\top}_{t} h(x_{t})\bigg), \\ \lambda_{t+1} &= \max\bigg( \lambda_{t} + \alpha g(x_{t+1}), 0\bigg), \\ \mu_{t+1} &= \mu_{t} + \beta h(x_{t+1}). \end{align*}xt+1​λt+1​μt+1​​=xt​−η(∇f(xt​)+λt⊤​g(xt​)+μt⊤​h(xt​)),=max(λt​+αg(xt+1​),0),=μt​+βh(xt+1​).​

These updates comprise the standard primal-dual algorithm, which maintains iterates for the dual λ\lambdaλ and μ\muμ in addition to the primal variable xxx. Nonetheless, the method may easily be extended, for example, by providing stateless estimations of λ(x)\lambda(x)λ(x) in place of a previous iterate λt\lambda_{t}λt​ when defining RtR_{t}Rt​ (paper pending).
