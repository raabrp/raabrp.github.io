Surprisal is All You Need

This short note extends the work of two prior papers:

  1. Hopfield Networks is All You Need by Ramsauer et. al.
  2. Energy Transformer by Hoover et. al.

The first of these papers recognizes that something like the attention mechanism can be derived from the minimization of a specific energy functional. The second extends this observation by proposing a second energy functional that corresponds to a different combination of attention units.

This note offers an interpretation of the energy functional as information-theoretic surprisal (i.e., how unlikely observations are), allowing us to interpret intermediate representations of the model in terms of the joint probabilities of given observations and competing hypotheses. The upshot is that, by fixing the energy functional and changing how these probabilities are represented, we can easily generate new mechanisms that share a theoretically-grounded interpretation. Given new, probabalistic interpretations of intermediate quantities, we may also be able to device improved methods for training the model.

Conclusion

The thing that’s “new” in this presentation is that we consider the distribution over “hypotheses” {A1,A2,...,An}\{A_{1}, A_{2}, ..., A_{n}\}{A1​,A2​,...,An​}, conditioned on the observation of ξ\xiξ, in a model-agnostic way: −Hi(ξ)=1βlog⁡Pr⁡(Ai,ξ).-H_{i}(\xi) = \frac{1}{\beta}\log \Pr(A_{i}, \xi).−Hi​(ξ)=β1​logPr(Ai​,ξ). That is, we are free to choose any means of calculating Hi(ξ)H_{i}(\xi)Hi​(ξ) or, alternatively, Pr⁡(Ai,ξ)\Pr(A_{i}, \xi)Pr(Ai​,ξ). For example, if we were to use multi-layer perceptrons instead of bilinear forms, could we save on the number of transformer layers used by contemporary architectures?

For any means of calculating Hi(ξ)H_{i}(\xi)Hi​(ξ) that we choose, using surprisal as the energy functional gives us the update step immediately:

ξt+1=ξt+η∇log⁡Pr⁡(ξt)=ξt+η1Pr⁡(ξt)∇Pr⁡(ξt).\begin{align*} \xi^{t+1} &= \xi^{t} +\eta \nabla \log \Pr(\xi^{t}) \\ &= \xi^{t} + \eta \frac{1}{\Pr(\xi^{t})} \nabla \Pr(\xi^{t}). \end{align*}ξt+1​=ξt+η∇logPr(ξt)=ξt+ηPr(ξt)1​∇Pr(ξt).​

Moreover, this update step can be readily generalized for non-Euclidean spaces, preconditioning matrices, momentum, etc.
