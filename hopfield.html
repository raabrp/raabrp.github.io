Surprisal is All You Need

This short note extends the work two prior papers:

  1. Hopfield Networks is All You Need by Ramsauer et. al.
  2. Energy Transformer by Hoover et. al.

The first recognizes that something like the attention mechanism can be derived from the minimization of a specific energy functional. The second extends this observation by proposing a second energy functional that corresponds to a different combination of attention units.

The contribution outlined here is an interpretation of the energy functional as information-theoretic surprisal (i.e., how unlikely observations are). The interesting consequences of this interpretation lie in the realization of an implicit probability distribution over unobserved hypotheses that is represented by the model.

Thoughts

What does it buy us to treat the distribution over implicit “hypotheses” A1,A2,...,AnA_{1}, A_{2}, ..., A_{n}A1​,A2​,...,An​ as “real”?

In the attention mechanism, this distribution is meant to represents the relevance between different tokens. That is, the distribution might be interpreted as “The probability that this past token applies the most”.

In Hopfield networks, where the index iii is often interpreted in terms of memories, Pr⁡(Ai∣ξ)\Pr(A_{i} \mid \xi)Pr(Ai​∣ξ) may correspond to “the probability that we’re in the same situation that generated memory AiA_{i}Ai​”.

In any case, unifying the energy functional with surprisal allows us to generate new schemes for approximating −Hi(ξ)-H_{i}(\xi)−Hi​(ξ) other than the linear function XijξjX_{ij} \xi_{j}Xij​ξj​. Upon doing so, we can immediately derive a corresponding update rule.

A unification based on information theory also allows us to interpret models in terms of variational principles akin to Free Energy Minimization. Indeed, given a Boltzmann distribution derived from fixed mean energy, log⁡Z\log ZlogZ is identically the Hemholtz Free Energy.
